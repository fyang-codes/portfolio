When Alarms Never Stop Screaming

UX Researcher | Critical Alarms Lab & TU Delft | April 2025 - Present

The Project Deep Dive

Ever wondered why ICU nurses seem to have superhuman abilities to work through constant beeping? Turns out, they're just really good at ignoring alarms - which is both impressive and terrifying. I'm currently deep in the world of alarm fatigue, trying to figure out why critical alerts get lost in the noise.

Picture this: you're in an ICU where alarms go off every 30 seconds. Some are life-threatening, some are just annoying sensor glitches. How do you tell the difference when your brain has learned to tune them all out? That's exactly what we're investigating.

Process

I've been hanging around Rotterdam Erasmus Hospital ICU units, convincing incredibly busy nurses to share their alarm horror stories through carefully designed CASTOR surveys. The recruitment process alone taught me that hospital timing is an art form - you learn to spot the 3-minute windows between emergencies.

  • CASTOR survey implementation with ICU nurses
  • ICU soundscape recordings and analysis
  • R for survey analytics on nursing stress levels
  • Python for soundscape dissection
  • SmartPLS for structural equation modeling

The Fun Really Starts With Data Analysis

I'm juggling R for survey analytics (discovering just how stressed these nurses really are), Python for dissecting soundscape recordings (yes, we actually recorded the ICU chaos), and SmartPLS for structural equation modeling. The goal? Finding the perfect formula that predicts when alarm fatigue hits hardest and what we can do about it.

Sound pattern analysis showing alarm frequency overlaps and interference patterns

What This Really Means

We're not just collecting data - we're trying to save lives by making technology that actually helps instead of overwhelms. Every data point represents a nurse's real struggle and a patient's safety.

Our early findings show that nurses develop highly personalized cognitive strategies to filter alarms. We've identified specific sound patterns that are most likely to be tuned out, and we're working on developing context-aware systems that can adapt their notification urgency based on the overall noise environment.
