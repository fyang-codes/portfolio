To Alarm, or Not to Alarm

Human factors research exploring ICU alarm fatigue and strategies for improvement.

Reasoning

Alarm fatigue in intensive care units (ICUs) is a critical issue affecting patient safety and healthcare worker effectiveness. This project examines the human factors behind alarm fatigue and proposes evidence-based solutions to mitigate its negative impacts on clinical decision-making and patient outcomes.

Process

This project followed a comprehensive human factors approach to analyze and address ICU alarm fatigue:

  • Conducted observational studies in multiple ICU environments
  • Interviewed healthcare professionals about alarm response behaviors
  • Analyzed alarm data to identify patterns and false positive rates
  • Developed prototypes for improved alarm management systems

Workflow analysis showing alarm triggers and healthcare response patterns.

Results

The research yielded several key insights and recommendations for addressing alarm fatigue in critical care settings:

  • Identified that 68% of ICU alarms did not require clinical intervention
  • Developed a priority-based alarm filtering system that reduced non-actionable alerts by 47%
  • Created guidelines for alarm customization based on patient-specific factors
