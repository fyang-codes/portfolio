Teaching Computers to See What Blind People Need to Know

Researcher | Koninklijke Visio | October 2024 - April 2025

The Real Story

Six months of diving into one of the most humbling research questions I've ever tackled: How do you help someone navigate a world they can't see, especially when that world is full of cars, bikes, and unpredictable Dutch pedestrians?

Working with Koninklijke Visio opened my eyes (pun intended) to how incredibly complex "simple" navigation really is. Think about crossing a busy Amsterdam street - you automatically process dozens of visual cues: car speeds, pedestrian movements, cyclist intentions, traffic light timing. Now imagine doing that with sound and touch alone.

Process

I designed a web-based experiment that was part detective game, part cognitive challenge. Participants had to identify hazards in dynamic street scenes while we tracked every click, every hesitation, every moment of uncertainty. The Google Scripts integration meant we could capture not just what people saw, but how long it took them to see it and where they looked first.

  • Web-based experiment design for hazard identification
  • Google Scripts for detailed interaction tracking
  • YOLO algorithm implementation for computer vision
  • Python analysis of temporal-spatial error patterns

The Human Element

Every data point represented someone's potential safety. When we found that participants consistently missed certain types of hazards, it wasn't just a statistical finding - it was a roadmap for better assistive technology. The temporal-spatial error patterns showed us exactly where and when people needed the most help.

The YOLO algorithm implementation was like teaching a computer to be a seeing-eye dog. We fed it thousands of street images, training it to recognize not just "car" or "person," but "car moving toward crosswalk" or "cyclist not paying attention."

What Made This Special

This wasn't abstract research. Every algorithm tweak, every data analysis session was driven by the knowledge that this work could mean the difference between someone safely crossing the street or not. The combination of cutting-edge computer vision and deeply human needs made every day feel meaningful.
